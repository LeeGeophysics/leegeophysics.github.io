What we study

Research

Six connected research areas, one goal: predictive models of the subsurface.

Our research sits at the intersection of geophysics, rock physics, and artificial intelligence. We develop quantitative workflows that turn seismic and well data into predictive 3D and 4D models of the subsurface, and we apply these models to carbon storage, induced seismicity, energy exploration, and geothermal systems. Much of our work centers on the Permian Basin of West Texas, with additional projects in the North Sea, Australia, and Korea.

Rock physics

Seismic rock physics and quantitative interpretation

We establish statistical and physics-based relationships between rock properties (total organic carbon, brittleness, permeability, mineralogy) and seismic properties (velocity, density, anisotropy, AVO attributes). These rock physics models enable quantitative interpretation of 3D seismic data for reservoir characterization, with extensive applications to the Wolfcamp Shale and other Permian Basin formations.

Machine learning

Machine learning and AI for the geosciences

We develop interpretable and physics-constrained machine learning methods for subsurface problems, including S-wave velocity log prediction with depth-constrained recurrent neural networks, Shapley value regression for explainable property estimation, and physics-informed neural networks (PINNs). Our goal is to make AI predictions accurate, explainable, and consistent with physical laws.

Geomodeling

3D and 4D geological and geomechanical modeling

We build basin-scale 3D models of elastic and geomechanical properties by integrating well logs, seismic data, and geostatistics. These models serve as digital twins of subsurface energy systems and support earthquake relocation, brittleness mapping, fluid injection analysis, and reservoir simulation across the Delaware Basin, Midland Basin, and Central Basin Platform.

CCUS

Carbon capture, utilization, and storage

As Co-PI of the DOE-funded Permian Regional CarbonSAFE Hub project, we characterize deep carbonate storage complexes in the Delaware Basin by integrating seismic interpretation, petrophysics, and static reservoir modeling. We also study time-lapse (4D) seismic monitoring of CO2 injection, including feasibility analysis for CO2-EOR and storage in the Permian Basin and plume imaging research at the Sleipner site in the North Sea.

Induced seismicity

Induced seismicity and subsurface monitoring

We investigate the links between fluid injection and induced earthquakes in West Texas. Building on our work with the Texas Seismological Network (TexNet) at the Bureau of Economic Geology, we construct 3D velocity and brittleness models that improve earthquake relocation and illuminate the geomechanical conditions controlling seismicity in the Permian Basin.

Geothermal

Geothermal energy and emerging applications

In collaboration with Hanyang University, we delineate geothermal prospects in hot sedimentary aquifers of the Cooper-Eromanga Basin, Australia, using seismic inversion, geostatistical modeling, and a geothermal reservoir quality index. We also explore how geothermal heterogeneity influences CO2 storage capacity.

Infrastructure

Facilities and software

Our lab operates GPU-accelerated high-performance Windows workstations and high-performance computing clusters that support deep learning, seismic inversion, and basin-scale 3D modeling, funded through the UT System STARs program. Students gain hands-on experience with industry-standard software through academic partnerships, along with well log and seismic data access through regional operators and software vendors.
